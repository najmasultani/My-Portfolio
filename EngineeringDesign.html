Engineering Design Projects

As an engineering science student at UofT Engineering, I have had the privilege of working on three captivating design projects. Through these experiences, I have been able to demonstrate my expertise, foster creativity, and gain valuable insights into the world of innovative product design.

The projects I have worked on include:

Blind Curling Training Device

During my Praxis II course project at UofT Engineering Science, my team (Joey, Kevin, Ria, Alice, and I) developed an innovative device to enable blind individuals to participate in curling independently. The device empowers blind curlers to learn and practice the sport without the need for a sighted guide. It incorporates four key components: an input camera above the curling sheet to capture images, a stone detection system using OpenCV software to translate these images into stone coordinates, an audio output through a loudspeaker to convey the stone's location, and a tactile map remote for target selection. With real-time feedback on shot types and stone positions, our solution makes curling more accessible and enjoyable for the blind community, fostering greater inclusivity in the game.

Vision Vapor Stopper

During my Praxis I design project, my team (Alex Kurk, Harry Chen, Natalie Lim, and I) created the "VisionVapor Stopper." It's a simple clip that attaches to any mask and prevents glasses from fogging up. By redirecting airflow away from the glasses' lenses, it ensures clear vision while wearing a mask. The "VisionVapor Stopper" is a user-friendly and effective accessory that enhances comfort and convenience for those wearing both glasses and masks. With its clever design, our clip offers a hassle-free solution, allowing individuals to go about their daily activities without worrying about foggy lenses. By addressing this common inconvenience, our "VisionVapor Stopper" exemplifies our commitment to creating innovative and practical solutions to real-life challenges.

CIV102-Bridge

As part of my CIV102 course project, my team (Nabeth, Samuel, Gabriel, and I) designed and built a small-scale box girder bridge using matboard. The goal was to create a bridge that could support the maximum load while matching the estimated failure load. We used MATLAB to find the best bridge geometry through iterative calculations. The final bridge successfully supported over 400 N of load, proving our teamwork and problem-solving skills. It was a hands-on application of engineering knowledge and reinforced the value of collaborative design processes.
